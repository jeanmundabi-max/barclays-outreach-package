Marketing Mix Modelling (MMM)

What it is: A statistical model that looks back at 2 to 3 years of spend and sales data and works out how much each marketing channel contributed to revenue — controlling for seasonality, price changes, competitor activity, and economic conditions.

Banking analogy (use this with Kirsty):
"Imagine a corporate lending portfolio. At year end, revenue is up 15%. Which products actually drove that — and which were just riding a rising market? MMM asks exactly the same question for marketing spend. Without it, you give credit to the biggest channel. With it, you find out the mid-size channel has three times the marginal return."
Sports analogy (use if the conversation is warmer):
"A football squad finishes third. The striker scored 20 goals — but 8 were penalties. The midfielder played every game and won possession. MMM is the end-of-season analysis that separates the actual contributors from the ones who were in the right place at the right time."

Land it with: "The output is a budget allocation recommendation — grounded in data, not instinct."

Incrementality Testing

What it is: A controlled experiment that measures how many sales or conversions would have happened anyway — without the marketing spend. The incrementality is the difference between what happened with it and what would have happened without it.

Banking analogy (use this with Kirsty):
"A relationship manager calls a client and they renew their mortgage the next week. Did the call cause the renewal — or were they going to renew anyway? Incrementality answers that. You hold back a matched group of similar clients, make no contact, and compare renewal rates. The gap is the true effect. Same principle — except for marketing spend rather than RM activity."
Music analogy:
"Take a track that sounds great. Remove one instrument and listen again. Did it get worse? By how much? That is incrementality — measuring the true contribution by removing the element and observing the difference. If the song sounds the same without the guitar, the guitar was not doing anything."

Land it with: "It is the only way to know if we are actually causing the sales — or just showing up near them."

Econometrics

What it is: The statistical discipline that isolates cause and effect from large, messy datasets — controlling for everything that is not the variable you are testing.

Banking analogy (Kirsty will get this immediately):
"It is what actuaries do with insurance risk — applied to marketing spend. An actuary has 20 years of loan default data and needs to know which variables actually predict default, not just correlate with it. Econometrics does the same for marketing: isolates the true contribution of spend from a rising market, a competitor going quiet, or a product price change that happened to coincide with the campaign."

Land it with: "The output is a number a CFO can put in front of the board: for every pound we spent, here is what we got back — and here is how confident we are in that number."

Attribution

What it is: The process of assigning credit for a sale or conversion to the marketing touchpoints that contributed to it — a customer may have seen a TV ad, then searched, then clicked an email before buying.

Banking analogy:
"A corporate deal closes. The relationship manager brought the client. The product specialist structured it. The credit team approved it. The MD did the introduction. Who gets the credit — and the bonus? Attribution is that conversation — but for marketing channels. And the answer matters because it determines where the budget goes next year."
Sports analogy:
"The striker scores. But the midfielder played the through ball and the winger started the move from deep. Attribution decides whether the goal credit goes entirely to the striker, is shared across the chain, or weighted by who made the decisive contribution. Last-click attribution is giving all the credit to the striker. MMM is working out who actually made the goal happen."

Land it with: "If you give all the credit to the last touchpoint, you defund everything that built the intent in the first place."

Brand vs Performance / The Long and the Short

What it is: Brand investment builds awareness and consideration over months and years. Performance investment converts that consideration into sales immediately. Both are necessary. The question is the right ratio.

Banking analogy (this one will resonate deeply with Kirsty):
"Short-term lending vs long-term relationship banking. A high-interest short-term product generates immediate revenue. A long-term client relationship builds someone who holds five products and refers three colleagues. Performance marketing is the short-term product. Brand is the relationship. Both have their place in the portfolio — but if you only optimise for immediate return, you hollowed out the relationship book without realising it. Binet and Field's research across 5,000 campaigns proves the same thing: the optimal split is roughly 60% brand, 40% performance. The brand does the groundwork. The performance campaign harvests it."

Land it with: "Moments of Progress is the brand investment. The mortgage and current account campaigns are the performance harvest. You need to measure both — but with different metrics and different time horizons."

How to know the analogy landed: She nods, or she says "that makes sense." Do not extend the analogy further. Move immediately to the business outcome or the Barclays application. The goal is to make her feel informed, not tutored.

Listen and Be Authentic
STAR: Manchester contact centre · 11% retention improvement
▾

If Anna already probed this story in Round 1, use the Santander Backup card instead.

Predicted questions:
"Tell me about a time when listening to others significantly changed your approach."
"Describe a situation where you had to be authentic even when the message was difficult."

Situation

At BT/EE, the SMB marketing team had been running CRM retention campaigns for 18 months with flat results. The working assumption was that the message was wrong or the targeting was off. I was about to recommend a campaign refresh — new creative, new targeting, new budget.

Task

Before spending any budget on creative or media, I wanted to understand why customers were actually leaving. The data showed who was resigning. It did not show why.

Action

Instead of building a brief, I went to the Manchester contact centre and spent a full day listening to actual resign calls. What I heard changed everything. Customers were not leaving because of the product or the price. They were confused. The CRM email language and the agent language were completely different — same moment, different offers, different tone. Customers did not know which to trust.

I came back and presented this to the team, not through a slide deck, but by playing back three anonymised call recordings. I said: "Before we redesign the campaign, listen to this." The room went quiet. Nobody had heard this before. I then proposed a fix that had nothing to do with creative: align the contact centre scripts with the CRM messaging. Same language, same offers, same timing.

Result

11% improvement in customer resign rates. Not from better emails. From closing the gap between what customers heard in marketing and what they heard on the phone. The fix cost nothing. The listening was the work.

Barclays hook

"At Barclays scale, the gap between what marketing says and what the bank delivers is a reputational risk as much as a retention problem. I would apply the same discipline: go to where the customer experience actually happens before recommending any marketing solution."

Listen and Be Authentic — Backup
STAR: Santander product challenge · Authentic over comfortable
▾

Use this if the Manchester story was already heard or probed in Round 1.

Situation

A senior product team leader at Santander was presenting projected ROI figures for a campaign that my models showed were 40% overstated. They had used best-case assumptions across every variable. Their team had been promised the campaign. Raising it publicly would embarrass them. Staying silent was easier.

Task

Challenge the numbers honestly without destroying the relationship. The product lead was senior to me. Being authentic here meant telling them a difficult truth in a way they could hear it.

Action

I requested a private meeting before the group budget review. I laid both sets of numbers side by side and said clearly: "I think these projections carry more risk than the base case suggests. I am not saying the campaign is wrong. I am saying we need to plan for the realistic scenario." I then offered a path forward: approve 60% now, gate the remaining 40% at week 4 based on in-flight data. When they pushed back, I held the position calmly: "If the data proves the model wrong, the remaining budget is yours immediately. I would rather protect your budget than gamble it."

Result

The gated approach was approved. At week 4, in-flight data confirmed the base case. The remaining 40% was redirected to a higher-performing campaign. The product lead said the gate review approach was something they adopted for future planning. The working relationship was stronger for the honesty, not weaker.

Key phrase to land

"I believe transparency builds trust. I would rather have a difficult conversation early than let bad data drive a bad decision."

Situation

When I proposed the single source of truth dashboard at BT, three functions — Marketing, Finance, and Commercial — each had their own spreadsheets, their own definitions, and years of habit. The proposal to replace all of that with one shared system was not popular. Each function feared they would lose visibility into what mattered to them. The instinct was to mandate it from above. I did not have that authority. I had to create belief in something they could not yet see.

Task

Get three independently minded, sceptical teams to commit to a shared system before it existed — without mandate.

Action

Instead of presenting a plan first, I built a prototype. Two weeks of weekend work. One month of data. A Tableau dashboard that pulled from all three function's numbers. Then I booked separate one-to-ones with each function lead.

In each meeting I did not present the dashboard. I asked one question first: "What is the one number you look at every Monday morning that tells you whether the previous week was good or bad?" Then I pulled up the dashboard and showed them that exact number, live, already in the system. For Marketing: cost per lead. For Finance: campaign spend versus plan. For Commercial: pipeline contribution.

When they could see their most important number in the shared system, the objection changed from "why should I give up my spreadsheet?" to "can you add the breakdown by product?"

Result

All three functions committed within three weeks. The tiger team was formed. The system is still running three years later. No mandate was required. The prototype did the energising.

Barclays hook

"In a bank where Marketing, Risk, Finance, and Decision Intelligence all have legitimate stakes in measurement data, you cannot mandate alignment. You have to show people something real and let it pull them in."

Note: Anna heard the BT transformation story as the Change & Transformation STAR. This is a different angle — it focuses on the alignment process (definition wars), not the transformation outcome. The result is different. The story feels fresh.
Situation

When I mapped data sources for the BT/EE SMB dashboard, I discovered that Marketing, Finance, and Commercial were not just using different spreadsheets. They were using different definitions for the same words. "Cost per acquisition" meant three different things. "Lead" had four definitions. "Campaign attribution" had two entirely different methodologies. Every joint meeting was built on this definitional chaos — and nobody had named it.

Task

Before building anything, get three teams with competing interests and different functional loyalties to agree on a shared language. Not to change how they thought about their work, but to agree on what the words meant when they were in the same room.

Action

I ran a three-session working series. Not "let's agree on the dashboard." I framed it as "let's agree on what we are measuring."

Session one: individually with each function — map every metric, what it means, what decision it informs. Session two: all three together with a side-by-side comparison of their definitions. Not to argue about who was right. To identify where the gaps were. I said: "Here is what Marketing means by cost per acquisition. Here is what Finance means. Here is what Commercial means. All three are valid internally. The question is what we use when we are all in the same room."

Session three: we agreed on 12 shared definitions. Each team kept their own internal metrics. But for joint conversations, there was one agreed set. I documented it as a one-page glossary and got all three function leads to sign off before I built a single line of the dashboard.

Result

The 7 definition mismatches were resolved before the build started. When the dashboard launched, the numbers were trusted on day one because the definitions had been agreed in advance. The glossary is still the onboarding reference for new analysts across all three functions.

Barclays hook

"At Barclays, this role sits at the intersection of the CMO office, Decision Intelligence, Finance, and Marketing Planning. My first job here would be to build the glossary, not the dashboard. Alignment on language comes before alignment on numbers."

Develop Others
STAR: The analyst who could not influence · From reporter to advisor in 6 months
▾

Predicted questions:
"Tell me about a time you developed someone in your team."
"How do you approach coaching people who are technically strong but lack broader business skills?"

Situation

At Santander, one of my team of 4 analysts was technically excellent. Strong SQL, sharp with data, accurate in every report. But she was invisible in stakeholder meetings. Product directors would ignore her findings or override them with gut feel. She came to me frustrated: "I give them the numbers and they do whatever they wanted to do anyway. What is the point?"

Task

She was right that the data was being ignored. But the problem was not the data — it was how she was presenting it. She was reporting, not recommending. She was telling stakeholders what happened without telling them what to do about it. Develop her from analyst to business advisor.

Action

I started with one change: every report had to end with a recommendation, not a summary. Not "CPA went up 12% this month" — but "CPA went up 12% this month. My recommendation is to pause the underperforming creative and reallocate to the channel showing the strongest return. Here is the projected saving if we act this week versus next."

Then I coached her on the meeting dynamic. We did dry runs before every major stakeholder presentation. I would play the product director and interrupt with objections: "Why should I believe this model?" "The sales team says something different." She learned to hold her position with data, not just repeat the number louder.

The third intervention was exposure. I started bringing her to budget review meetings and letting her present her section — not with me leading and her supporting, but with her leading and me present if needed.

Result

Within 6 months, product directors were going to her directly before budget meetings to stress-test their projections. The dynamic flipped: instead of her presenting findings they ignored, they were seeking her out to validate their assumptions. She was promoted within a year. The skill she developed was not technical. It was the ability to translate data into decisions in a room full of people who had not asked for her opinion.

Barclays hook

"The measurement team at Barclays will only be as effective as its ability to influence decisions in rooms where Marketing, Finance, and Decision Intelligence all have a voice. I would invest the same time developing the team's ability to advise, not just report."

Incrementality — The Full Answer
How to design a geo holdout test for Moments of Progress TV
▾
Use this if they ask: "How would you design an incrementality test?" or "How would you measure whether TV is actually working?"

"Incrementality testing answers a question that attribution cannot: did the ad cause the conversion, or would they have converted anyway?

Here is how I would design the first test at Barclays. I would start with Moments of Progress TV — the highest spend channel. If you can prove TV incrementality, every budget conversation changes."

Step 1: Region Matching

Pull 18 months of historical data — mortgage applications, current account openings, app downloads — at ITV region level. Match regions on: population, existing Barclays market share, historical application rates, demographic profile, and branch density. Create 6 to 8 matched pairs using nearest-neighbour or propensity scoring. Decision Intelligence validates the methodology.

Example pairs: West Midlands vs East Midlands. South Wales vs South West. Glasgow vs Edinburgh.

Step 2: Test Design

Control group: Full Moments of Progress campaign running — TV, BVOD, OOH, digital.
Holdout group: Suppress TV only. Keep digital and OOH running. The only variable is TV.
Duration: 6 weeks minimum. Brand campaigns take longer to show effect than performance campaigns.

Step 3: Measurement

Primary KPIs: mortgage applications, current account openings, app downloads by region. Compare lift between matched pairs. Calculate incremental applications attributable to TV. Divide by TV media spend in those regions. You now have a true cost per incremental application — not attributed, caused.

Where Tests Break

  • Region spillover — if ITV regions overlap, the holdout gets exposed. Need clean geographic boundaries.
  • Statistical power — you need enough historical variance to detect a signal. Barclays volumes are high enough. This is not a blocker.
  • Ending early — stopping a test because early results look good is the most common mistake. Commit to the full duration.
Honest framing on experience: "At Santander I worked with an econometrics agency that ran MMM. I implemented the framework connecting model outputs to budget decisions. I have not personally executed a geo holdout end to end — but I have designed the methodology, I understand the statistical requirements, and this is what I would build in the first 90 days in partnership with Decision Intelligence."

Attribution vs Incrementality — The One-Liner

"Attribution tells you which touchpoints were present before a conversion. Incrementality tells you which touchpoints caused it. Attribution is descriptive. Incrementality is causal. For budget decisions that matter, you need the causal answer."

Month 3 will not prove it — but it will show whether the signal is moving in the right direction.
  • Identify one quick win that can be actioned immediately — one finding from the first 90 days that the team can act on without waiting for the full 12-month proof. Measurement that produces no decisions is not measurement. It is reporting.
  • Set the IPA Award ambition: the north star for the measurement architecture is a case study that proves Moments of Progress closed the satisfaction gap. The IPA Effectiveness Awards are the industry benchmark for this kind of brand transformation proof. Barclays already won with Moneyverse on econometric ROI. The next submission should win on something harder — and more impressive.

Deliverable by end of month 3: First live MoP measurement report, first cohort signal, and a roadmap showing what the measurement architecture will prove — and by when.

How to close this answer

"The honest answer is that the first 90 days will be shaped by what I find when I get there. But the framework I have just described is not speculative — it is based on reading the research Barclays has already published about Decision Intelligence, Acacia Avenue, and the strategic priority of deepening customer relationships. The data infrastructure is already there. What I would build is the measurement architecture that connects it into one system — and the stakeholder alignment to make sure everyone trusts the numbers that come out of it."
